§ 04.1
Foam & cushion stack

Single-density foam is always a compromise — firm enough to sit on bottoms out your hips when sleeping; soft enough to sleep on sinks when you sit. The spec is dual-layer throughout: a firm base layer carries body weight; a softer comfort layer goes on top. Both seat panels use a 2″ + 1″ stack; the mattress panel uses a thicker 3″ + 1″ stack so the bulk of your sleeping weight lands on the deepest foam.

Two numbers define each foam layer and are the only specs that matter when shopping across vendors. PCF (pounds per cubic foot) is density — how durable and long-lasting the foam is. Higher PCF resists body impressions. ILD (indentation load deflection) is firmness — how many pounds of force it takes to compress a 4″ sample by 25%. Higher ILD = firmer. Ask any supplier for “1.8 PCF, 36 ILD polyurethane” and they’ll know exactly what you mean regardless of what they call it on the shelf.

§ 04.2
Upholstery & moisture stack

The fabric on hand — soft white furniture cloth, blanket-weight — needs reinforcement and moisture protection before it sees van conditions. The construction is a five-layer stack from outside to inside: fabric · BellBloc 68 liner · batting · sew foam · main foam. BellBloc handles moisture, mold, and mildew; it also stiffens the fabric enough that separate iron-on interfacing isn’t needed. Channel pleating runs front-to-back at 5″ intervals: nine channels with 3″ flat margins on each side, sewn through fabric + BellBloc + sew foam.

Channel layout — 48″ bench width

3″ flat margin · nine channels at 5″ on-center · 3″ flat margin. Stitched front-to-back through fabric, BellBloc 68 liner, and sew foam. Same channel pattern on seat, back, and future mattress panels for continuity.

YoshiOS
Onboard Compute · Telemetry

A custom onboard computer system for Yoshi, rendered through an amber monochrome CRT salvaged from a 1986 Magnavox Videowriter 350. The architecture binds vehicle telemetry, navigation, audio routing, and IMU orientation data into a single 1-bit amber dashboard.

The runtime is Electron on a fanless industrial mini-PC, drawing live Victron data over MQTT through the Cerbo GX hub. Currently running on simulated data; the simulator transparently swaps to live feeds when the Cerbo IP and Portal ID are populated in main.js.

Fig. B System architecture. Cerbo GX collapses the entire Victron stack into a single MQTT feed over Ethernet. 03 / 06

Sound & Insulation Strategy
Acoustic · Two Stacks Compared

Two complete acoustic / thermal stacks, captured from notes shared by Matt. Same layering logic, different price-and-weight tier at every layer. Premium · Heavy-Duty was selected — the weight penalty is worth it on a van where the engine sits directly behind the cabin. The optimized stack is retained below for reference.

Every layer does one of three jobs: damping (CLD mat stops the steel ringing), blocking (CCF + MLV decouples and absorbs airborne noise), or filling (Thinsulate handles thermal R-value plus interior absorption). Confusing one for another is the most common mistake — you can’t substitute layers between jobs.

Feature ✓ Premium · Heavy-Duty Optimized · Lightweight
Target use Maximum noise & heat isolation — road trip quiet, highway cruising comfort Excellent damping with ~40% less added weight
Floor stack Damplifier Pro (25–40% coverage) → Luxury Liner Pro full coverage (foam down) → ½″ marine ply subfloor → vinyl / laminate finish Noico 80 mil (40–50%) → ¼″ closed-cell foam (EZ-Cool / Thinsulate) → ⅛″ MLV sheet (0.5 lb/ft²) → ⅜″ birch ply → vinyl finish
Walls / sliding door Damplifier Pro + Thinsulate + vapor barrier + interior panel Noico 80 mil + 1″ Thinsulate (SM600L) + vapor barrier + panel
Ceiling Damplifier + 1″ Thinsulate + vapor barrier 1″ Thinsulate only — lighter, good heat resistance
Rear firewall Double layer: Damplifier + Luxury Liner Pro Damplifier + ½″ CCF + ⅛″ MLV
Wheel wells Damplifier + Spectrum spray or LLP patches Damplifier + LizardSkin Sound Control
Total weight (est.) ~110–130 lbs ~60–75 lbs
Thermal R-value ~R-4 — high radiant + conductive barrier ~R-3.5 — very good for the weight
Airborne noise (STC) 25–30 dB 18–22 dB
Materials cost $900–1,200 $450–600

T3-specific considerations

Rear firewall treatment carries unusual weight on a Vanagon — the engine sits directly behind the bench, so that wall is your back when seated. The double-layer premium spec is doing real work there.

The engine deck lid is a known T3 weak point: original sound-damping disintegrates with age and is commonly replaced with EVA foam tiles plus a CLD layer. Not in this table but worth pricing in.

Weight matters more on a Vanagon than on a modern Sprinter. The optimized column is probably the more honest target for a T3 already weight-sensitive when new.
